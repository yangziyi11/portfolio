Push-to-talk App redesign

This is a B2B project between Samsung and a client company to design a Push-to-talk (PTT) application for first responders.

PROJECT OVERVIEW

Push-to-talk applications can leverage features from smart phones, and deliever more useful features to first responders than the traditional LMR devices.

This is a B2B project where Samsung designs the Push-to-talk application for another company. The project you are seeing here is focusing on user experience improvement for the push-to-talk action and related screens.

IMPROVING THE CORE EXPEREINCE

Our team took over the old version of design (Release 1) from HQ, and started from Release 2. Below is the conversation view screen of the old design:

To understand the current user experience and potential areas for improvement, we asked our client's help to invite us to focus group sessions, and beta trial interviews. We collaborates with their UXR agency, and identified below problems:

PTT panel too small

Users find it hard to identify current floor status. The product owner also want to make the PTT panel larger so that it will be easier for users to operate.

How do I speak

User don't understand how to speak. They are suprised to find sometimes it's tap to speak and sometimes they need to press and hold the PPT button.

Complicated call logic

Users reported it's hard to start a new call when they are currently on an active call.

(1) New conversation view structure

During focus group interview and 1:1 interview, users reported that they seldomly use text messaging function, but they use on the push-to-talk function much more often. They also find it hard to tell the push-to-talk status due to the small PTT button and subtext.

Through competitor analysis of Zello, EPTT and Verizon Push-to-talk, we found our major competitors all have big PTT panel.

To provide a larger PTT panel, below are 2 options we considered, and we decided to go with the tab design after weighing pros and cons.


a. Tab

Pros: Users will get a larger PTT panel with clearer floor status. Bigger PTT key will also make it easier for users to operate in urgent situations, where limited attention can be assigned to our app.

Cons: Users need to do one more swipe to go to the conversation panel. Also, users will not be able to read conversation history as they speak.


b. Expandable PTT panel

Pros: Previous structure is kept, while we also provide a solution for users with more needs.

Cons: If most users want to use the expanded view, they need an extra step to do it. More importantly, in later design phase, another new feature was decided to use the swipe up/down interaction for conversation view, which making them conflicts with each other.

(2) Consistent Push-to-talk

Comparing to traditional LMR services, we have the concept of "Call" in our Push-to-talk application. Due to the previous KPI design, the previous designer decided to use "Tap" interaction for call start, because it will decrease the time between users interaction to the time point the call actually starts.

Users complained that they sometimes need to press and talk to speak, but sometimes just need to tap to speak. It's very confusing.

We discussed with client stakeholders with user research findings, and also involved our PM into discussion. Our client agrees to our suggestion for making call activation and floor granting interaction consistent with "press and hold". KPI was modified for better user experience.

After that, I worked with our GUI designer to redefine the style for PTT button status. We use filled solid style to indicate the button is being pushed, and use linear style to indicate the button isn't been pressed.
